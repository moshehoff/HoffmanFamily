The Zitserman Family

This paper includes updates as of October 2024.

This uncommon Jewish family name was my grandmother’s maiden name. I have not found its origin in any source, however I learned that in Yiddish a person who sits on a panel or in judgment is a besitser/בעזיצער from the word “zits”/זיץ “to sit”. Thus a zitserman/זיצערמאן or as it is sometimes written – zaitserman - must have been a dayan/דיין - a member of a rabbinical court. In European languages constructions using the root words for seats, sitting (and even thronus) such as chairman, assizes, seat of government, a sitting, etc, are common.

Bershad: The Zitserman Ancestral Home

Bershad (ברשד) was a small town in the Podolia Governate of the Russian Empire, located in what is now the Vinnytsia Oblast, Ukraine. Like many shtetls in the Pale of Settlement, Bershad had a substantial Jewish community that formed the backbone of the town’s commercial and cultural life.


The interior of the Bershad’s old synagogue

The Zitserman family was part of this community, with Aaron Zitserman described on his daughters’ death certificates as a business proprietor—a common occupation for Jews in the region, who were generally barred by law from owning agricultural land.

Jewish life in Bershad, as in other Podolian shtetls, revolved around the synagogue, religious education, and tight-knit family networks. The town was subject to the same antisemitic restrictions and periodic violence that afflicted Jewish communities throughout the Pale of Settlement, particularly during the pogroms of the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

Aaron Zitserman and Elka Vestil

Aaron Zitserman of Bershad married Elka Vestil.

A Digression into This Name

I have the name Vestil from the death certificates of two of his daughters (Tob’l and Pessy) where it is their mother’s family name. The death certificate of Mariam notes that her mother was named Elka. This we know to be true. It follows that Vestil can only be the family name. Each of the three daughters – Tob’l, Pessy and Mariam - bore a daughter named Elke – they were Alyce Hoffman, Olga Schwarz and Elke Greenberg – in line with Jewish naming conventions. On his daughters’ death certificates Aaron was described as a business proprietor.

Vestil: This rare Jewish family name appears to be unique to Russia and Moldavia. A search of the Jewish Genealogy databases and of the Shoah victims listed by Yad Vashem has not turned up much of relevance.

2. Frida (~1887–1955)

Frida died in Odessa. She married Mocha Pesach of Olgopol. Olgopol was a town near Vinnitsa in the Podolia oblast. After about 1941 Olgopol became part of Transnistria, and was no longer in the Ukraine or the USSR.

They had six children, of whom three (Charna, Fanya Feiga, and Bronya Bracha) were war widows and one Yaakov was killed in action.

Charna. Came out from Odessa as an old lady to Israel and died in Israel. She married (given name not known) Pesach. He was conscripted and fell in the world war.

Manya May. Died in Odessa. She married Boris Granovski. He was a lieutenant, who fell at Stalingrad in 1942. Their daughter was Nelli Granovski, b. 1935, died in Israel ~2010.

Fanya Feiga. She married Pesach Zvesdina. Came out from Odessa as an old lady to Israel and died there. I know nothing of her.

Bronya Bracha b. ~1918 in Olgopol. Came out from Odessa as an old lady to Israel and died there. Bronya m. Michael Solgan, b. in Olgopol. Michael was a captain (4 pips) in the Red Army. He fell in 1942 (before the battle of Stalingrad). Their son Yefim (Fima) was born in 1938. He was a civil engineer whose interest was in hydraulics. He left Odessa and came to Israel.

Arkadi Aaron Pesach b. ~1917. He was a colonel (polkovnik) in the Red Army who fought as far as Berlin. In later life he lived in St. Petersburg. I know nothing more.

Yaakov (Yankele) Pesach b. ~1925, killed in action, aet. 17 years in 1943.

In 1928, my grandfather Morris Hoffman nominated Frida and Leib and their six children for immigration to Australia (Australian National Archives, item 8183056). This did not eventuate. This was in the time of the antisemitic repressive USSR.

6. Yaakov (Yankele) (~1880s–1913–1916)

Birth order not known. Leah Dzienciol said that he was a dayan in Bershad. Yaakov’s great grandson Alex Igoudin “found” us in August 2017, and he has united our families and given us the following family details: Yankel Zitserman was probably born in the 1880s. He married Rachel (no last name survives). Yakov died sometime between 1913 and 1916. Yakov might have enlisted in the Russian army during WWI, in which Russia participated from 1914 to 1917, and perished then.

The Sydney Zitserman Family

We know of a Zitserman family, that left Russia and came to Sydney in 1980. My cousin Ruth (Uncle Wolf’s daughter), by chance, met them. In August 1997 Philip Dzienciol visited and interviewed them with the intention of learning their family history. They are descended from Yoel (Yankele) Zitserman of Bershad, and Gittel. Philip concluded that Yoel and our great grandfather Aaron were brothers. This has been subsequently confirmed by Lane. Philip also thought that Gittel and our great grandmother Elke were sisters.
